# Section I - Understanding the Business

# **Chapter 1** **- MD7 Business Model**

The best Account Executives don't sell lease amendments or buyouts-they help two parties with different objectives find a future they can both confidently agree to.

## **Chapter Objective**

By the end of this chapter, an Account Executive should understand:

- What MD7 actually sells.
- Who MD7 represents.
- Why customers engage with MD7.
- Why landlords agree to renegotiate existing agreements.
- Why this is **not** a traditional sales process.
- How to mentally frame every customer interaction.

## The MD7 Mission

MD7 exists to help telecommunications providers maximize the long-term value of their wireless infrastructure portfolio.

Rather than constructing new towers or acquiring new sites, MD7 specializes in optimizing existing real estate assets by renegotiating lease agreements, acquiring lease interests, and reducing long-term occupancy costs while preserving network reliability.

The company's role is to create value for both the carrier and the property owner by identifying opportunities where a revised agreement better aligns with each party's long-term objectives.

## Who MD7 Represents

MD7 works on behalf of telecommunications providers. Examples include:

- AT&T
- Verizon
- T-Mobile
- Regional wireless operators
- Infrastructure investment groups
- Tower companies (where applicable)

The Account Executive represents the interests of the client while maintaining a professional, transparent, and respectful relationship with the property owner.

**Mindset:** You are not negotiating _against_ the landlord-you are helping both parties arrive at a sustainable long-term agreement.

## What MD7 Actually Sells

Many new Account Executives mistakenly believe they are selling a lease amendment or a buyout. Neither is true. The contract is only the mechanism. The real value depends on the path.

### **Path One - Lease Optimization**

The customer receives:

- Continued predictable income
- Reduced uncertainty
- Long-term contractual stability
- Continued relationship with the carrier

The carrier receives:

- Reduced occupancy expense
- Improved portfolio economics
- Longer-term site stability

### **Path Two - Lease Buyout**

The customer receives:

- Immediate liquidity
- Financial flexibility
- Estate simplification
- Elimination of future uncertainty

The carrier (or investment partner) receives:

- Long-term control
- Reduced future costs
- Greater flexibility for future infrastructure decisions

## The Three Stakeholders

Unlike many sales environments, there are three distinct interests that must be balanced.

### **The Carrier**

Primary objectives:

- Lower long-term costs
- Maintain network continuity
- Improve capital efficiency
- Reduce future contractual risk

### **The Property Owner**

Primary objectives:

- Protect income
- Preserve fairness
- Maintain trust
- Maximize financial outcome
- Protect family interests

### **MD7**

Primary objectives:

- Facilitate agreement
- Maintain professionalism
- Reduce friction
- Create mutually acceptable outcomes
- Protect long-term client relationships

A successful Account Executive understands all three perspectives before attempting to influence any one of them.

## Why Landlords Say "Yes"

A common misconception is that landlords only agree because the financial offer is attractive.

In reality, decisions are often driven by broader life circumstances.

Common motivations include:

- Retirement planning
- Estate planning
- Desire for certainty
- Reducing future risk
- Debt elimination
- Business investment
- Simplifying family finances
- Property redevelopment
- Health considerations
- Liquidity for future opportunities

The agreement becomes a solution to a larger life objective.

## Why Landlords Say "No"

Resistance rarely stems from numbers alone.

More often, it reflects uncertainty or emotional attachment.

Common reasons include:

- Fear of making a mistake
- Attachment to long-term passive income
- Family expectations
- Distrust of corporations
- Belief that waiting will produce a better offer
- Loss aversion
- Lack of urgency
- Unclear understanding of future network changes

Understanding the source of resistance is more valuable than immediately attempting to overcome it.

## The Role of the Account Executive

The Account Executive is not a closer. The Account Executive is an educator, consultant, negotiator, and guide.

Success comes from helping property owners make informed decisions-not from convincing them to accept a proposal they do not understand.

The best AEs create clarity before they create commitment.

## Business Outcomes

Every conversation should ultimately move toward one of two outcomes:

### **Outcome A**

A mutually beneficial lease amendment that provides long-term stability for both parties.

### **Outcome B**

A mutually beneficial buyout that converts future lease income into immediate financial value.

If neither outcome can be **framed** as an improvement to the customer's situation, the opportunity should not progress.

## Chapter Summary

- MD7 optimizes existing lease portfolios rather than acquiring new sites.
- The Account Executive represents the carrier while treating the property owner as a long-term relationship, not an adversary.
- Every proposal should solve a business or personal problem, not simply reduce costs.
- Success is built on trust, education, and informed decision-making-not pressure.

## What Great AEs Notice

- The landlord talks more about **their future** than the lease itself.
- They describe the rent as "retirement income" versus "extra income." Those are very different conversations.
- They mention family members, accountants, or attorneys early-an indicator that additional stakeholders will influence the decision.
- They ask "Why now?" before asking "How much?" They are seeking context before valuation.
- They refer to the tower as "my tower" or "our tower." This often signals emotional ownership beyond contractual relationships.

# **Chapter 2 - The Economics & Psychology Behind Telecom Leases**

**"Property owners rarely negotiate based on the contract in front of them.**

**They negotiate based on the future they imagine that contract will create."**

A lease is not merely a legal agreement. For many property owners, it represents security, identity, independence, and long-term planning. Great Account Executives recognize that they are not negotiating a contract-they are helping someone evaluate the future that contract supports.

## Chapter Objective

By the end of this chapter, an Account Executive should understand:

- Why carriers seek to optimize existing lease portfolios.
- How wireless networks evolve over time.
- Why a long-standing lease is not necessarily a permanent one.
- Why landlords often overestimate the certainty of future lease income.
- How to discuss network evolution without creating fear.
- How to position change as prudent financial planning rather than loss.

## Why Carriers Renegotiate Existing Leases

Wireless infrastructure is not static.

Carrier priorities evolve as technology, customer demand, regulations, and network architecture change.

A lease negotiated twenty years ago may no longer reflect today's market conditions, operating costs, or strategic priorities.

Carriers periodically review their portfolios to:

- Reduce long-term occupancy expenses.
- Standardize lease terms across large portfolios.
- Increase flexibility for future network changes.
- Improve capital efficiency.
- Align legacy agreements with current business objectives.

For the carrier, optimization is a routine part of managing a national infrastructure portfolio.

## **Executive Perspective**

Every dollar saved on recurring lease expenses can be reinvested into:

- Network modernization
- Spectrum deployment
- Customer experience
- Capacity expansion
- Future infrastructure investments

The objective is not simply to lower rent-it is to improve the long-term efficiency of the network.

## Why Some Towers Become Less Strategic

One of the most important concepts an Account Executive must understand is that not every site maintains the same strategic importance forever.

Changes that influence a site's value include:

- Advances in wireless technology.
- Increased network density.
- Changes in population distribution.
- Carrier mergers.
- Spectrum reallocations.
- Infrastructure upgrades.
- New construction in surrounding areas.

A tower that was mission-critical twenty years ago may serve a different role today.

Conversely, some sites have become even more valuable over time.

The goal is never to predict a specific site's future-it is to acknowledge that network planning is dynamic rather than permanent.

### Teaching Moment

Instead of saying:

**"The tower may not stay forever."**

Reframe the conversation:

**"Most property owners naturally assume that because the lease has been in place for decades, it will continue indefinitely. In reality, carriers continuously evaluate thousands of sites to ensure the network reflects today's needs rather than yesterday's."**

This introduces uncertainty without making speculative claims.

## Understanding the Property Owner's Perspective

While the carrier views the lease as an operating expense, the property owner often views it as a financial asset.

That distinction drives almost every negotiation.

To the property owner, the lease may represent:

- Retirement income
- Supplemental household income
- College funding
- A family legacy
- Financial independence
- Stability during economic uncertainty

When an Account Executive suggests changing the agreement, the customer may hear:

**_"You're asking me to change my future."_**

Understanding this emotional translation is essential.

## The Hidden Cost of Certainty

Many landlords mentally categorize lease payments as guaranteed income.

In reality, the value of the lease depends on factors outside either party's direct control, including: value of the lease depends on factors outside either party's direct control, including:

- Future network strategy
- Technology changes
- Contract renewals
- Market conditions
- Regulatory environments
- Property ownership changes

The objective is not to create fear, but to encourage a more complete view of the asset.

### Teaching Moment

**"When most people evaluate a lease, they compare today's monthly payment with tomorrow's monthly payment. Few stop to consider the value of flexibility, liquidity, or reducing uncertainty over the next twenty years."**

That insight reframes the conversation from **price** to **portfolio management**.

## Two Different Ways to Create Value

Once the landlord understands that the lease is an asset rather than simply a monthly payment, two strategic paths emerge.

### **Path One - Lease Optimization**

Best suited for owners who value:

- Ongoing income
- Stability
- Predictability
- Long-term ownership

The conversation centers on preserving cash flow while improving long-term certainty.

### **Path Two - Lease Buyout**

Best suited for owners who value:

- Immediate liquidity
- Retirement planning
- Estate simplification
- Debt reduction
- Investment opportunities

The conversation centers on converting a future income stream into present-day financial flexibility.

Neither path is inherently better. The best path is the one that aligns with the customer's long-term objectives.

## Chapter Summary

- Carriers optimize lease portfolios as part of responsible network management.
- Wireless infrastructure evolves continuously; long-standing leases are not automatically permanent.
- Property owners often view the lease as a life asset rather than a commercial contract.
- Great Account Executives discuss risk thoughtfully, not fearfully.
- The objective is to help customers evaluate which financial path best supports their future.

## What Great AEs Notice

- The customer talks about "security" more often than "money."
- They describe the lease using family language ("our retirement," "my kids' inheritance") rather than investment language.
- They ask about long-term certainty before discussing valuation.
- They compare today's situation to the past ("It's been here 40 years") instead of considering future possibilities.
- They seek reassurance that they're making a prudent decision, not simply a profitable one.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating network evolution as a scare tactic instead of an educational conversation.
- Speaking in absolutes about future carrier decisions.
- Assuming every customer is motivated by maximizing price.
- Focusing on lease terms before understanding the role the income plays in the customer's life.
- Confusing agreement with understanding-customers often nod politely before they've actually reframed their perspective.

## Manager Coaching Questions

- Did the AE explain _why_ carriers optimize portfolios in a way the customer could understand?
- Did the AE connect network evolution to prudent financial planning rather than fear?
- Can the AE articulate how the customer views the lease-as income, legacy, investment, or security?
- Did the conversation uncover how the customer defines value before introducing either path?
- Did the AE leave the customer thinking differently about the asset than when the conversation began?

This chapter intentionally lays out the psychological foundation for the rest of the playbook. Before an AE learns **how** to negotiate, they first need to understand **how the other side interprets value**.

# Chapter 3 - Understanding the Property Owner

**"People rarely make financial decisions based solely on money. They make decisions based on what the money represents."**

A monthly lease payment may appear to be a financial asset, but to the property owner it often represents something far more personal: security, independence, family, legacy, or peace of mind. Great Account Executives learn to negotiate around meaning-not just numbers.

## Chapter Objective

By the end of this chapter, an Account Executive should understand:

- The different types of property owners encountered.
- How each owner typically thinks about the lease.
- What motivates each ownership profile.
- The emotional significance of long-term passive income.
- Why different property owners require different conversation strategies.
- How to identify the customer's primary decision driver within the first two conversations.

## Every Lease Has a Story

One of the biggest mistakes new Account Executives make is assuming every lease is simply another transaction. It isn't.

Every lease has its own history. Some were signed by the current owner. Others were inherited. Some helped pay off the farm. Some funded children's education. Some became retirement income after a spouse passed away. Others are barely noticed because they represent a small portion of the owner's wealth.

The lease is never just a contract. It has a place in the owner's life story.

Great Account Executives become curious about that story before discussing the contract itself.

## Common Property Owner Profiles

### Family Farm Owner

Often:

- Multi-generational land ownership
- Deep emotional attachment
- Conservative financial mindset
- Long-term thinking
- High trust required

Primary Motivations

- Protect family legacy
- Preserve land ownership
- Stable income
- Simplicity

Conversation Style: Respect history, avoid rushing. Ask about the property before discussing the tower.

### Retiree

Often:

- Fixed income
- Predictable budgeting
- Risk sensitive
- Wants fewer complications

Primary Motivations

- Financial certainty
- Peace of mind
- Estate planning
- Healthcare planning

Conversation Style: Focus on options rather than pressure. Explore how they define financial security.

### Commercial Property Owner

Often:

- Business-oriented
- Evaluates investments objectively
- Comfortable negotiating

Primary Motivations

- Return on investment
- Cash flow
- Asset performance

Conversation Style: Lead with economics. Expect detailed questions.

### Family Partnership

Often:

- Multiple decision makers
- Different priorities
- Longer sales cycle

Primary Motivations

- Fairness
- Family agreement
- Preserving relationships

Conversation Style: Identify all stakeholders early. Never assume one family member speaks for everyone.

### Trust / Estate

Often:

- Attorney involvement
- Trustee approval
- Formal process

Primary Motivations

- Fiduciary responsibility
- Documentation
- Compliance

Conversation Style: Highly structured. Expect legal review.

### Municipality

Often:

- Committee decisions
- Public accountability
- Procurement rules

Primary Motivations

- Community benefit
- Compliance
- Budget

Conversation Style: Professional. Process-driven. Documentation matters.

## What the Monthly Check Really Means

A lease payment is rarely just income.

Ask yourself:

**What role does this money play in this person's life?**

Possible answers:

- Mortgage payment
- Retirement income
- Vacation fund
- Emergency savings
- Family inheritance
- Farm operating expenses
- College tuition
- Supplemental income
- Psychological security

That answer changes the entire conversation.

### Teaching Moment

Instead of asking:

**"How important is this income?"**

Try:

**"Everyone uses these lease payments differently. Some people hardly notice them, while others have built long-term plans around them. I'm curious-what role has this lease played for you over the years?"**

This encourages storytelling instead of defending a position.

## The Four Primary Decision Drivers

After enough conversations, most motivations fall into four broad categories.

### Security

The customer values stability above all else.

Indicators:

- Talks about predictable income
- Risk averse
- Wants guarantees

Best Path: Usually, Lease Optimization.

### Opportunity

The customer sees capital as something to deploy.

Indicators:

- Investments
- Business ownership
- Real estate purchases
- Entrepreneurial mindset

Best Path: Usually Buyout.

### Legacy

The customer thinks beyond themselves.

Indicators:

- Children
- Grandchildren
- Estate
- Family history

Conversation: Explore what legacy means. Sometimes income matters, Sometimes simplicity matters.

Never assume.

### Simplicity

The customer wants fewer responsibilities.

Indicators:

- Aging
- Health
- Multiple properties
- Administrative fatigue

Best Path: Often Buyout.

## Stakeholders Beyond the Property Owner

Influencers may include:

- Spouse
- Adult children
- Financial advisor
- CPA
- Estate attorney
- Business partner
- Trustee
- Siblings
- Property manager

Your job is not to avoid these stakeholders. Your job is to identify them before they become surprises.

### Teaching Moment

Try:

**"When people make decisions about long-term assets like this, they often like to involve someone they trust-a spouse, family member, accountant, or attorney.**  
**How do decisions like this typically get made in your** **family?"**

This normalizes additional stakeholders instead of implying the customer lacks authority.

# **Chapter 4 - The MD7 Challenger Mindset**

**"People don't resist change because they dislike new ideas. They resist change because they haven't yet found a future they trust more than their present."**

The Account Executive's responsibility is not to "overcome objections." It is to help property owners see possibilities they had not previously considered. The objective isn't to win an argument-it's to reduce uncertainty until the best decision becomes clear.

## Chapter Objective

By the end of this chapter, an Account Executive should understand:

- Why traditional sales approaches fail in lease optimization.
- How the Challenger methodology applies to emotionally anchored assets.
- The difference between educating and persuading.
- How to establish credibility before introducing a different perspective.
- How to create productive tension without damaging trust.
- When to challenge assumptions-and when not to.

## Why Traditional Selling Fails

Most sales training assumes the customer is already interested in solving a problem.

In MD7, that assumption is usually false.

Many property owners begin the conversation believing:

- Nothing is wrong.
- The lease is working.
- There is no reason to change.
- More time is always better.
- Saying "no" costs them nothing.

If an AE immediately discusses numbers, discounts, or buyout offers, the customer mentally defaults to protecting the status quo.

The first challenge is **not** selling the solution.

It is helping the customer reconsider the problem.

## Challenger Principle

People rarely change because they are given information.

They change because they begin asking themselves different questions.

## Earn Permission Before You Teach

A conversation is not a lecture. It's a dialogue.

Before introducing a new perspective, establish credibility through curiosity.

Examples:

Instead of:

**"I'm calling because we'd like to discuss your lease."**

Try:

**"Before we discuss why I'm reaching out, I'd like to understand a little about your experience with the property. How long has it been in your family?"**

Curiosity earns permission.

Permission earns attention.

Attention creates influence.

## The Challenger Sequence

Every meaningful conversation should follow the same progression.

### Step 1 - Understand

Learn before teaching.

Questions focus on:

- The property
- The owner's goals
- Their history
- Their plans
- Their relationship with the lease

Never interrupt a story. Stories contain motivations.

### Step 2 - Validate

Before introducing a different perspective, acknowledge theirs.

Examples:

- "I can understand why you'd feel that way."
- "Given your experience, that makes sense."
- "I'd probably view it similarly if I'd owned the property that long."

Validation lowers defensiveness. Agreement is not required. Respect is.

### Step 3 - Teach

Introduce one commercial insight. Never overwhelm the customer. One insight is enough.

Example:

**_"One thing that surprises many long-term property owners is how often carriers evaluate their entire portfolio-not because a site is performing poorly, but because technology and network strategy continue to evolve."_**

Pause.

Let the customer respond.

### Step 4 - Explore

Ask questions that allow the customer to process the insight.

Examples:

- "Had you ever thought about it that way?"
- "How does that compare to how you've viewed the lease over the years?"
- "What questions does that raise for you?"

The objective is reflection-not agreement.

### Step 5 - Guide

Only after understanding the customer's reaction have you earned the right to discuss possible paths.

The recommendation should feel like a logical conclusion to the conversation-not a predetermined destination.

## The Difference Between Pressure and Productive Tension

Poor salespeople create urgency through pressure. Great Account Executives create urgency through clarity.

Pressure sounds like:

**"This offer won't last forever."**

Productive tension sounds like:

**"Whether you choose to make a change or not, it's worth understanding the long-term implications before deciding that keeping everything exactly the same is automatically the lowest-risk option."**

One creates resistance. The other creates thoughtful consideration.

## Reframing Without Fear

Never use fear as leverage. Instead, broaden the customer's perspective.

Avoid:

**"The tower could disappear tomorrow."**

Prefer:

**"Most long-term leases have experienced changes over time. Our goal is simply to help property owners evaluate today's opportunities with a full understanding of how the industry continues to evolve."**

The customer should leave better informed-not alarmed.

## Every Conversation Has One Goal

Many AEs think the goal of each call is to advance the deal. The real goal is simpler.

**Leave the customer thinking differently than they did before the conversation.**

If the customer's perspective changes, future progress becomes possible.

If it doesn't, additional meetings rarely matter.

## The Trust Equation

Trust is built through consistency.

Customers evaluate four things:

### Competence

**"Do they know what they're talking about?"**

### Transparency

**"Are they being honest with me?"**

### Respect

**"Do they genuinely care about my situation?"**

### Intent

**"Are they trying to help me make the best decision-or just close a deal?"**

A deficiency in any one area weakens the relationship.

## Conversation Framework

Every conversation should quietly answer four questions in the customer's mind:

- **Can I trust this person?**
- **Do they understand my situation?**
- **Have they shown me something I hadn't considered?**
- **Would I feel comfortable speaking with them again?**

If the answer to any of these is "no," the conversation ended too early or moved too quickly.

## Chapter Summary

- Challenger begins with curiosity, not confrontation.
- Great AEs earn the right to introduce new perspectives.
- Productive tension comes from clarity, not pressure.
- One meaningful insight is more powerful than ten facts.
- The objective of every interaction is to expand the customer's thinking before asking them to make a decision.

## What Great AEs Notice

- The customer asks follow-up questions instead of defending their position. This signals curiosity replacing resistance.
- The customer begins using phrases like "I never thought about that," "That's interesting," or "Let me think about that." These indicate a successful reframe.
- The customer's emotional tone shifts from guarding the past to exploring the future.
- Silence after an insight often means the customer is processing-not rejecting-the information.
- Customers who begin asking "what if" questions are mentally moving from certainty toward evaluation.

# **Chapter 5 - The Anatomy of an MD7 Conversation**

**"The goal of the first conversation is not to negotiate a lease. It's to earn the privilege of having a second conversation."**

Great Account Executives don't close deals on the first call.

They lower resistance. Build trust. Create curiosity. Earn permission.

Every successful negotiation begins with a conversation that doesn't feel like a negotiation.

## Chapter Objective

By the end of this chapter, an Account Executive should understand:

- The purpose of every stage within a customer conversation.
- How to naturally guide a discussion without sounding scripted.
- How to uncover motivations before discussing proposals.
- When to teach.
- When to ask questions.
- When to stop talking.
- How to leave every conversation with a mutually agreed next step.

## The Conversation Pyramid

One mistake many AEs make is trying to climb the pyramid too quickly.

Proposal  
Recommendation  
Financial Discussion  
Business Objectives  
Personal Objectives  
Discovery  
Rapport & Trust

Every level depends on the one beneath it. Skip a layer, and the conversation becomes unstable.

## Stage 1 - Establish Comfort

### Objective

Reduce the natural skepticism that accompanies an unexpected call.

Before the customer decides whether they trust MD7, they decide whether they trust **you**.

The opening minutes should communicate:

- Professionalism
- Respect
- Confidence
- Curiosity
- Patience

### What You're Really Selling

Not the lease. Not the buyout. You're selling: **"This conversation is worth having."**

### Example Flow

Introduce yourself.

Explain why you're calling.

Acknowledge the unexpected nature of the conversation.

Ask permission to continue.

Then... Stop talking.

### Customer Mindset

Who is this?

Can I trust them?

What do they want?

Am I about to be sold something?

### Exit Gate

Customer willingly continues the conversation.

## Stage 2 - Build Context

Before learning what the customer wants... Understand what exists today.

Topics:

- Property history
- Lease history
- Ownership
- How long they've owned it
- How they became involved
- Changes over time

Never rush. Stories build trust.

### Teaching Moment

Don't ask:

**"How long have you owned the lease?"**

Instead:

**"I'd love to understand the story behind the property. Has it always been in your family?"**

People remember stories. Not questionnaires.

### Exit Gate

You understand:

- Ownership
- Property history
- Relationship to the lease

## Stage 3 - Discover Meaning

Most AEs ask about money. Great AEs ask about meaning.

Questions:

- "What role has the lease played over the years?"
- "What originally attracted you to keeping the agreement?"
- "Has the lease become more important over time, or less?"
- "What would change if the lease income disappeared tomorrow?"

That last question isn't about fear. It's about dependency.

### Exit Gate

You understand what the lease represents. Not just what it pays.

## Stage 4 - Introduce Insight

Only now do you earn the right to teach. Introduce one idea. Not ten.

Examples:

- Network evolution
- Changing infrastructure
- Capital flexibility
- Risk diversification
- Portfolio optimization

Then... Wait. Silence is part of the conversation.

### Exit Gate

Customer reflects, asks questions, and explores.

## Stage 5 - Explore Future Goals

Now connect today's lease to tomorrow's plans.

- "What are your long-term plans for the property?"
- "Have you thought much about estate planning?"
- "Is maintaining monthly income the priority-or having options?"
- "Do your children have interest in keeping the property?"
- "What would you like this asset to accomplish over the next 10-20 years?"

Now you're discussing life planning. Not telecom.

### Exit Gate

Future goals clearly identified.

## Stage 6 - Determine Best Path

Now... (Only now), Should the AE begin thinking:

Lease Optimization? Buyout? Neither?

The recommendation should emerge naturally from discovery. Never feel predetermined.

### Decision Matrix

| **Customer Priority**         | **Recommended Path**                |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Stable monthly income         | Lease Optimization                  |
| Retirement planning           | Usually Buyout                      |
| Estate simplification         | Usually Buyout                      |
| Debt elimination              | Buyout                              |
| Property held for generations | Explore before recommending         |
| Passive supplemental income   | Lease Optimization                  |
| Capital for investments       | Buyout                              |
| Uncertainty about future      | Lease Optimization discussion first |

### Exit Gate

Internal recommendations are established.

The customer agrees that the recommendation is worth exploring.

## Stage 7 - Define the Next Step

The conversation does not end with: "I'll send you something."

It ends with mutual commitment.

Examples

- Financial review.
- Proposal.
- Family meeting.
- Attorney discussion.
- Property documentation.
- Follow-up appointment.
- Always leave with:
- Date.
- Time.
- Purpose.
- Expected attendees.

## Exit Gate

Specific next meeting scheduled.

## Reading the Conversation

One of the most important skills an AE develops is recognizing **where** the customer is emotionally.

| **Customer Behavior**         | **Interpretation**    | **AE Response**                    |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Short answers                 | Low trust             | Slow down. Build rapport.          |
| Long stories                  | Comfort increasing    | Listen. Don't interrupt.           |
| Future-oriented questions     | Considering change    | Introduce options.                 |
| Price questions early         | Anchored to economics | Continue discovery before quoting. |
| Asks about process            | Mentally progressing  | Explain next steps.                |
| Introduces spouse or children | Stakeholders emerging | Welcome broader discussion.        |

### Conversation Milestones

The first conversation is successful if the customer says at least one of these:

- "I hadn't thought about it that way."
- "That's interesting."
- "I'll have to think about that."
- "My wife would probably want to hear this."
- "I'd like to understand how that works."
- "What happens next?"

These are not closing signals. They're **thinking signals**. Thinking signals come before buying signals.

## What Great AEs Notice

- The customer becomes more conversational as trust grows.
- They stop defending the current lease and start exploring future possibilities.
- They begin asking "what if" instead of saying "no."
- They volunteer personal goals without prompting.
- They ask about process before price-a strong indication they're envisioning moving forward.

## If This... Then That

| **If This Happens...**                     | **It Likely Means...**                      | **Then Do This...**                                                                           |
| ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Customer interrupts frequently.            | They don't yet trust the conversation.      | Slow down. Ask more questions. Validate before teaching.                                      |
| Customer shares personal history.          | Trust is increasing.                        | Stay with the story. Don't pivot prematurely.                                                 |
| Customer asks, "Why are you calling now?"  | They're seeking context, not rejecting you. | Explain the portfolio review and the purpose behind the outreach before discussing solutions. |
| Customer says, "I'm listening..."          | They're open but cautious.                  | Introduce one insight, then pause.                                                            |
| Customer says, "Let me talk to my family." | The decision process has expanded.          | Ask who should be involved and offer to include them in the next conversation.                |

## Common Mistakes

- Treating the call as a sales presentation instead of a dialogue.
- Asking twenty questions in a row without adding value.
- Introducing pricing before understanding motivation.
- Filling every silence.
- Mistaking politeness for commitment.
- Ending the conversation without a clearly defined next step.

## "Conversation Heat Meter"

| **Signal**                                                     | **Temperature** | **Meaning**                                     |
| -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| One-word answers                                               | 🔵 Cold         | Stay in rapport and context-building.           |
| Storytelling begins                                            | 🟢 Warm         | Continue discovery; avoid pitching.             |
| Customer asks "why" or "how"                                   | 🟡 Engaged      | Introduce a Challenger insight.                 |
| Customer discusses future plans                                | 🟠 Invested     | Explore which path aligns with those goals.     |
| Customer invites family, attorney, or advisor into the process | 🔴 Committed    | Transition into planning and formal next steps. |

Don't advance the conversation because _you've covered your script_-advance it because the customer's engagement level indicates they're ready.

## Manager Coaching Questions

- Did the AE earn trust before introducing new perspectives?
- What personal objective did the customer reveal?
- Did the recommendation logically emerge from discovery?
- What was the customer's biggest shift in thinking?
- Was a specific, mutual next step established?

# **Chapter 8 - Mastering Discovery Conversations**

**"The quality of your recommendation will never exceed the quality of your discovery. Customers don't object to good recommendations-they object to recommendations made before they feel understood."**

Every question should accomplish one of three things:

- Build Trust
- Create Understanding
- Reduce Uncertainty

If a question accomplishes none of those, **don't ask it.**

## The Six Primary Decision Drivers

Every customer is unique.

However, after thousands of conversations, most decisions can be traced back to one dominant motivation.

The six primary drivers are:

- Security
- Legacy
- Liquidity
- Opportunity
- Simplicity
- Control

Most customers will exhibit one primary driver and one secondary driver. Your role is to identify both before making a recommendation.

Reducing uncertainty is often more important than maximizing value.

- Regret

## The Five Rules of Challenger at MD7

### Rule 1 - Understand before educating

If you don't understand their world... Your insight won't matter.

### Rule 2 - Teach one insight

Not five. One good insight changes perspective.

Ten insights become a lecture.

### Rule 3 - Let silence work

Most AEs answer their own Challenger insight. Don't. Teach. Pause. Let them think.

### Rule 4- Challenge assumptions

Never challenge people.

Wrong: **"You're looking at this incorrectly."**

Better: **"Many property owners initially see it that way until they begin looking at how today's network decisions are different from twenty years ago."**

### Rule 5 - End with curiosity

Not persuasion. Always follow insights with questions.

## Scenario 1 - "The Lease Has Been Here 30 Years"

**What They Really Mean:** Stability equals permanence.

Don't Say **"So much has changed."**

### Challenger Insight

Pause. Then Ask:

**"What do you think has allowed your site to remain valuable all these years?"**

Why It Works: They explain the value. Instead of defending it.

Pause. Then Ask:

The conversation shifts from money to decision philosophy.

The customer contributes effort to the process.

The customer prepares for a final decision.

- Final negotiations.

### **Exit Gate**

A decision timeline has been mutually agreed upon.

# **Chapter 16 - Coaching the MD7 Way**

## **Executive Insight**

**"The purpose of coaching is not to provide answers. It is to improve the quality of the decisions your people make when you're not in the room."**

Many managers become bottlenecks because every difficult opportunity requires their involvement.

Elite managers do the opposite.

Every coaching conversation increases the Account Executive's ability to solve similar situations independently.

The goal of coaching is not dependency.

The goal is judgment.

# **Chapter Objective**

By the end of this chapter, leaders should understand:

- How to coach thinking instead of tactics.
- How to diagnose root causes instead of symptoms.
- How to improve judgment.
- How to conduct opportunity reviews.
- How to use questions instead of directives.
- How to identify development opportunities.
- How to create a coaching culture.

# **The Coaching Philosophy**

Traditional Sales Management

Inspect activity.

Elite Sales Leadership

Improve thinking.

Traditional

"Did you ask for the sale?"

Elite

"How did the customer make decisions during the conversation?"

Traditional

"What objection did you get?"

Elite

The manager's job is not to rescue opportunities.

The manager's job is to improve future opportunities.

# **Coaching Framework 1 - Opportunity Reviews**

Managers should rarely begin with:

# **Coaching Framework 3 - Skill Diagnosis**

Every performance issue belongs in one of five categories.

| Confidence   | They know the answer but hesitate.                  |
| Discipline   | They know, can do it, but don't do it consistently. |

Managers often misdiagnose.

Most "skill" issues are actually judgment issues.
